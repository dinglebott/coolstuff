Big Bang

The Big Bang remains one of the most mysterious moments in our Universe's history to date. This is mainly because no one was around to see it happen. However, we do have some ideas...

Contents of the Universe

At first, there were almost equal amounts of matter and antimatter, which annihilated each other almost instantly. However, there was a TINY bit of matter left over, roughly 0.0000033%. In other words, for every 30 million kg of antimatter, there was 30 million and 1 kg of matter! That leftover matter makes up every galaxy, star, and planet we see today.

Cosmic Microwave Background

It took 380,000 years after the Big Bang for the Universe to become transparent. This was when the very first atoms formed, turning the Universe from an opaque plasma into a gas. (Before this it was too hot to form stable atoms.) The light that travelled the Universe at this point, originating all the way from the Big Bang, can still be seen today!

Heatmap of the CMB. This pattern can be seen everywhere in space, a background of microwave from every direction at the same time.

Nature of the Big Bang

NOTHING is known about the actual singularity we spawned from. Our two existing frameworks to explain all of reality are general relativity and quantum mechanics. The two theories are fundamentally different in how they conceptualise our reality, and CANNOT agree with each other.

Usually, relativity is applicable at cosmic scales (stars and planets), while quantum mechanics is used at quantum scales (electrons and quarks), and thus they don't overlap. However, singularities like the Big Bang are a rare circumstance where BOTH relativistic and quantum effects are significant. This is where our theories fail us, because we have NO CLUE how to do it.

The only known places in the Universe with similar conditions are the singularities inside black holes. Which are a bit hard to reach. So we currently have nothing concrete to really study in this field...
